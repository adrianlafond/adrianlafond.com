"That's from six months ago," I said to Puckwood.

"Who's Zadie seven-seven-nine?"

I ran on quick search on the username. Full name, photo, last known address flashed onto my screen. Puckwood pulled a wild U-turn in the middle of the block and floored it down an empty side street at eighty miles per hour, rocketing past stop signs and red lights. We weren't actually in all that much of a hurry; it just made him feel better, I suppose.

"I bet she's lying," said Puckwood.

"Who?"

"Madeleine. Like, wishful thinking. I've never heard of anybody not turning, anywhere."

"Supposedly there are cases," I said. "Rare but true. You never see them because we grab them the moment we hear about them. Off the streets and into labs. She's right about that."

"The rest of us need to live," said Puckwood, "so some people are just going to have to suffer."

"That's one way to look at it."

"They says it's been with us for years, lying dormant, waiting for the right moment. Whatever the hell it isa virus, they say. Like the flu, but worse."

The sky ahead, between the buildings on either side of the road, suddenly became as dark as dusk.

"Fire," said Puckwood.

And just like that, we were stuck. The roads had been empty only minutes before but now it seemed every car in New York City was parked in front of us. The city no longer had anything like rush hour, nor peak times, nor off-peak timesjust an unpredictable mishmash where anything could happen at any time at all.

We were too far away to see any actual flames in any building or automobile. Smoke darkened the sky and that was all. Static belched out of the police scanner because, as usual, the network was down. Cars and trucks were parked or abandoned to a standstill blocking the path before us. Within seconds we were boxed in from behind as well.

"Whatever," said Puckwood.

We smoked cigarettes and reclined the seats and waited it out. Puckwood watched porn on his phone. At least he kept the volume down. His thing apparently was mostly straight normal stuffone man fucking one women. The man and the woman both always wore sacks over their heads.
